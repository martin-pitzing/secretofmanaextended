# Secret of Mana Revisited: Faction Bible

## Purpose

This document defines the major factions for the revisit, preserving the original roles where useful and explicitly marking the **new inputs added for the reinterpretation**.

Use this format while developing:

- `Original function`: what the faction does in the base game structure
- `Revisit input`: **what I am adding or changing**
- `Story uses`: how the faction should appear in plot, quests, and character arcs
- `Visual identity`: how the faction should read on screen
- `Conflict hooks`: what stories this faction naturally generates

## World-Level Premise

In the revisit, mana is not only magic. It is also:

- ecology
- memory
- legitimacy
- military leverage
- religious authority

That means every faction should want mana for a different reason.

## 1. Potos And The River Villages

### Original function

Potos is the opening village and moral baseline of the world. It introduces taboo, superstition, communal fear, and the cost of the sword being removed.

### Revisit input

**Added input: Potos is no longer just a tutorial village. It is the first example of how ordinary communities survive under mana instability.**

**Added input: The village elders maintain half-remembered containment traditions around the waterfall, but they no longer understand the full reason those rites exist.**

**Added input: Potos is part of a wider river-village network that shares old flood warnings, monster signs, and oral fragments about forbidden relics.**

### Story uses

- Gives Randi a real social wound when he is exiled.
- Shows how quickly fear turns into scapegoating when mana balance breaks.
- Establishes that local people are custodians of damaged truths, not simple peasants waiting for heroes.

### Visual identity

- wet cedar wood
- talismans over doors
- river stones carved with fading ward-signs
- practical homes with old ritual additions that villagers treat as ordinary architecture

### Conflict hooks

- some villagers want Randi dead or gone permanently
- others think the old taboos failed because the elders became complacent
- river communities begin disagreeing on whether temples should be reopened or sealed harder

## 2. The Water Palace Order

### Original function

The Water Palace and Luka anchor the early explanation of mana, seals, and the journey to restore the sword.

### Revisit input

**Added input: The Water Palace is not the single unquestioned center of spiritual authority. It is the most visible surviving temple order, but not the only interpretation of mana law.**

**Added input: Luka acts as a stabilizer and coordinator, but her authority is partly political because other temple orders believe the Water Palace has become too central.**

**Added input: The Water Palace preserves records, envoys, and ritual maps, making it as much an archive-state as a temple.**

### Story uses

- central quest dispatch and spiritual orientation
- source of incomplete truth rather than full exposition
- a place where the player learns that restoration is not ideologically neutral

### Visual identity

- pale stone
- controlled geometry
- aqueduct-like sacred channels
- attendants and scribes, not only priests

### Conflict hooks

- rival temple keepers accuse Luka of overreach
- palace archives contain censored or fragmented records about earlier mana wars
- the party must decide when to follow Luka and when to question her

## 3. Pandora Court

### Original function

Pandora introduces Primm, Dyluck, court pressure, war anxiety, and one of the first major human costs of the crisis.

### Revisit input

**Added input: Pandora is a court split into three active blocs: war command, dynastic conservatives, and reform-minded nobles who see the kingdom decaying from inside.**

**Added input: Dyluck's value is not just personal or romantic. He is politically important because he is one of the few commanders trusted by both soldiers and common citizens.**

**Added input: Primm is not merely rebelling against an arranged marriage. She is rejecting a whole system where marriages are used to secure grain, military aid, and magical legitimacy.**

### Story uses

- drives Primm's character growth
- gives the war a human and institutional face
- shows how corruption spreads through governance before it spreads through monsters

### Visual identity

- frayed banners
- beautiful interiors repurposed for wartime administration
- sealed districts and guarded sanctuaries
- visible contrast between noble spaces and exhausted public areas

### Conflict hooks

- whether Pandora should negotiate with the Empire to survive
- whether Dyluck was sent on a real mission or quietly sacrificed
- whether Primm can ever return home as a daughter of the court

## 4. Tasnica

### Original function

Tasnica serves as a major anti-imperial power and a later story counterweight to the Empire.

### Revisit input

**Added input: Tasnica is competent but overstretched, surviving through intelligence work, fortification, and carefully managed alliances rather than pure military strength.**

**Added input: Tasnica's leadership also exploits mana myths when useful, making it a necessary ally but not a morally pure one.**

**Added input: Jema's history with Tasnica ties the Gemma line to failed pre-war diplomacy and to old decisions that helped centralize temple knowledge.**

### Story uses

- broadens the world beyond local kingdoms
- introduces strategic thinking and statecraft
- lets the party see that "anti-Empire" does not equal "just"

### Visual identity

- disciplined architecture
- practical military storage
- map rooms and coded records
- less sacred ornament, more durable civic engineering

### Conflict hooks

- Tasnica wants the party's help but also wants control of information
- officers disagree on whether the Mana Sword should be treated as a weapon or symbol
- Primm and Randi may lose trust in allies who speak the language of necessity

## 5. The Empire Of Vandole

### Original function

The Empire is the main visible antagonist force seeking the Mana Seeds and ultimately the Mana Fortress.

### Revisit input

**Added input: The Empire is not monolithic. It contains four major blocs whose cooperation is unstable.**

**Added input: Bloc 1, the Imperial Court, wants dynastic permanence and symbolic world rule.**

**Added input: Bloc 2, the Expansion Command, wants roads, garrisons, ports, and resource corridors secured under one enforceable order.**

**Added input: Bloc 3, the Mana Engineering Bureau, wants controlled access to ancient technology and sees the old civilization as a technical inheritance, not a warning.**

**Added input: Bloc 4, the Unification Doctrine, is a zeal-driven faction convinced that fractured kingdoms and temple traditions are what doomed the world the first time.**

**Added input: Many civilians tolerate or support imperial rule because the Empire can temporarily deliver stability, anti-monster campaigns, and infrastructure.**

### Story uses

- primary military pressure across the middle game
- recurring antagonist presence in occupied settlements
- ideological mirror to the heroes: order, unity, and restoration pursued through domination

### Visual identity

- engineered camp layouts
- repeatable military signage
- transport wagons and mana instruments
- banners that read more like state branding than noble heraldry

### Conflict hooks

- officers competing over access to temple sites
- occupation policies that are beneficial in one town and brutal in another
- internal suspicion over how much control Thanatos actually has

### Subfaction: Imperial Court

#### Original function

Represents the Emperor and central authority.

#### Revisit input

**Added input: The court believes that sacred legitimacy can be rebuilt through total possession of relics, temples, and bloodlines.**

**Added input: Court advisers are obsessed with symbolic sovereignty, including who gets seen beside relics and which regions publicly submit first.**

#### Story uses

- court scenes, decrees, propaganda
- explains why occupation is theatrical as well as strategic

### Subfaction: Expansion Command

#### Original function

Military arm of imperial conquest.

#### Revisit input

**Added input: Expansion Command is practical and often contemptuous of mystics. Its officers want a world that can actually be supplied, taxed, and defended.**

**Added input: This bloc creates the most interesting gray-zone antagonists because some of its arguments are materially persuasive.**

#### Story uses

- roadblocks, garrisons, transport seizures, desert and port occupations
- recurring commanders with place-based authority

### Subfaction: Mana Engineering Bureau

#### Original function

Implied through the Empire's interest in the Fortress and ancient power.

#### Revisit input

**Added input: This bureau studies relics, temple conduits, corrupted cores, and mana-reactive architecture.**

**Added input: It is where the revisit can connect temples, magic systems, and the endgame fortress into one coherent chain of research and abuse.**

**Added input: Some bureau members are not loyal to the Emperor personally; they are loyal to discovery.**

#### Story uses

- dungeon laboratories
- reverse-engineered mana devices
- logs, experiments, and failed restorations

### Subfaction: Unification Doctrine

#### Original function

Not explicit in the original as a separate bloc.

#### Revisit input

**Added input: This is a new ideological faction inside the Empire. It teaches that plurality itself is the disease of the world.**

**Added input: It interprets temple independence, regional identities, and old spirits as obstacles to necessary unity.**

**Added input: This bloc gives the Empire a philosophy beyond conquest for its own sake.**

#### Story uses

- speeches, recruitment, justification for severe policy
- thematic pressure against the party's defense of local difference

## 6. Thanatos And The Necromantic Network

### Original function

Thanatos manipulates the Empire, abducts victims, drives possession, and hijacks the endgame.

### Revisit input

**Added input: Thanatos is not just a villain. He is the surviving intelligence of a worldview that treats bodies, grief, and kingdoms as vessels.**

**Added input: He operates through a distributed network of cult cells, compromised scholars, court intermediaries, and ritual scavengers.**

**Added input: He studies succession crises, mourning rites, and regions where memory is already unstable, because those are the easiest places to enter.**

**Added input: Dyluck and Phanna are not random targets. They are selected because each fills a different ritual and political need.**

### Story uses

- hidden hand behind escalating corruption
- bridge between human political conflict and metaphysical horror
- emotional engine for Primm's and Pandora's tragedy

### Visual identity

- ritual scars
- blackened altars
- bodies and spaces that feel inhabited by intent rather than merely haunted
- decayed grandeur instead of monster clutter

### Conflict hooks

- who is serving Thanatos knowingly versus unknowingly
- what records about him were deliberately erased by temple orders and states alike
- whether the Empire created the opening he needed or he created the Empire's final form

## 7. The Temple Orders

### Original function

The temples and their spirits are the structure through which mana restoration and spell progression occur.

### Revisit input

**Added input: Each temple has a local order, keeper lineage, or inherited civic relationship. The temples are not a single religion with different buildings.**

**Added input: Every temple order should represent a different answer to one question: should mana be shared, guarded, bargained with, localized, or forbidden?**

**Added input: Several temple orders distrust each other and interpret the Mana Sword differently.**

### Story uses

- makes every temple arc distinct
- provides ideological variety rather than repeated exposition
- ties progression to local story consequences

### Visual identity

- architecture driven by region and function
- unique keepers, textiles, markings, and ritual tools
- different storage, archive, and altar practices per site

### Conflict hooks

- one order may want resealing instead of restoration
- one order may have cooperated with imperial researchers under duress
- one order may blame the Gemma tradition for centralizing a burden no one should own

## 8. Dwarven Forges And Salvage Clans

### Original function

The dwarves and Watts support weapon reforging and connect the party to buried sites and practical craft.

### Revisit input

**Added input: The dwarves are not just blacksmith NPCs. They are salvage historians who understand that old mana civilizations survive in fragments, alloys, and machine ruins.**

**Added input: Forging is both technical labor and ritual risk. Reawakening old weapon paths may restore power, but also restore old obligations.**

**Added input: Different dwarf clans disagree on whether ancient weapons should be reforged at all.**

### Story uses

- grounds the myth in physical craft
- gives weapon progression narrative meaning
- creates a non-state faction with real historical knowledge

### Visual identity

- industrial warmth
- heavy tools and lifted relic pieces
- carved clan marks
- practical sacredness rather than priestly ceremony

### Conflict hooks

- whether the Mana Sword should be restored fully
- who owns relic metal recovered from dangerous sites
- whether Watts is respected, reckless, or both

## 9. Popoi's Lost People

### Original function

Popoi is an amnesiac outsider connected to magic and a hidden past.

### Revisit input

**Added input: Popoi's past should tie to a displaced or partially erased people affected by earlier mana catastrophes.**

**Added input: This does not need to become a giant new empire. It works better as a broken lineage, hidden enclave, or fragmented cultural memory scattered across regions.**

**Added input: Several factions know pieces of the truth and each has reshaped it for their own purposes.**

### Story uses

- deepens Popoi's identity arc
- connects late-game revelation to earlier worldbuilding
- lets magic have cultural stakes, not only mechanical ones

### Visual identity

- unfamiliar symbolic motifs that recur in temples and ruins before they are explained
- color accents and design forms that set Popoi apart from local human cultures

### Conflict hooks

- whether Popoi's people were victims, collaborators, or both
- whether recovering this history would destabilize temple legitimacy
- whether Popoi wants ancestry, belonging, or freedom from both

## 10. The Gemma Tradition

### Original function

Jema and the Gemma role guide the hero and connect the party to the sword's larger duty.

### Revisit input

**Added input: The Gemma tradition is less a noble order of pure guardians and more a burdened institution that monopolized dangerous knowledge.**

**Added input: Earlier Gemma figures made morally compromised choices: concealment, selective truth, and political bargains to keep the world stable.**

**Added input: Jema is loyal to the world, but not fully confident that the old system deserves to survive unchanged.**

### Story uses

- gives mentor figures ambiguity
- reframes prophecy as inherited damage control
- creates tension between duty and reform

### Visual identity

- practical relic-bearing travelers
- formal marks of office worn with visible age
- archives carried on the body: seals, blades, tablets, oaths

### Conflict hooks

- temple orders resent Gemma centralization
- rulers want Gemma endorsement for legitimacy
- Randi may become the first hero to break the role instead of merely fulfilling it

## Faction Relationship Matrix

Use this in plotting:

- Potos fears temple instability but depends on temple protection
- Water Palace coordinates restoration but is distrusted by some temple orders
- Pandora needs allies but is vulnerable to dynastic compromise
- Tasnica opposes the Empire but also manipulates information
- The Empire wants central control of mana, trade, and myth
- Thanatos exploits every institution that mistakes control for safety
- Dwarven clans preserve material truth better than courts do
- The Gemma tradition carries sacred authority and historic blame

## Chapter 1 Active Faction Pressures

The following pressures should be visible during Chapter 1:

- Potos villages are split between superstition, fear, and shame after the seal incident.
- Falls shrine custodians want the forbidden zone tightened, not opened.
- Luka and the Water Palace present restoration as necessary, but their authority already feels politically centralized.
- Pandora is already under internal court strain before Primm fully joins the larger journey.
- Imperial pressure is present through rumors, supply disruption, border anxiety, and indirect political influence rather than a full military set piece.
- Thanatos is not identified openly, but the player should feel that corruption events are coordinated rather than random.

## What To Highlight In Writing

When these factions appear in scenes, always highlight:

- what they fear
- what they think mana is for
- what sacrifice they believe is acceptable
- what truth they are hiding

## Best Immediate Writing Sequence

1. Write named leadership for Pandora, Tasnica, the Empire, and two temple orders.
2. Define three recurring imperial officers from different blocs.
3. Write Luka and Jema as partially aligned but not identical authorities.
4. Create one temple dossier where local keepers openly disagree with the Water Palace.
5. Build Primm's early scenes so faction conflict is personal before it becomes geopolitical.
