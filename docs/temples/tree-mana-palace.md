# Tree Palace / Mana Palace Dossier

- Temple ID: `temple_tree_mana_palace`
- Scope: second-half dossier
- Labels used:
  - `Original anchor`: material already present in *Secret of Mana*
  - `Added series input`: motif or structure lifted from classic Mana-series feeling
  - `Added temple turn`: new twist for this revisit

## Original anchor

- Final sacred ascent.
- Highest mythic concentration of the story.
- Linked to the Mana Tree and the world's closing revelation.

## Added series input

- Borrow the older Mana-series feeling that the Tree is not just a symbol but a living burden.
- Make the finale feel like a confrontation with a civilizational system, not only a boss.
- Let the site feel older than history and populated by traces of past guardianship.

## Added temple turn

- The Tree Palace reveals that the temple network was built not only to protect Mana, but also to distribute the burden of maintaining a world that repeatedly consumes chosen guardians.
- The final truth is not just "protect the Tree." It is "decide whether this system deserves to continue."
- The palace's chambers show that every age has outsourced its survival to someone or something that could not consent to the cost.
- The finale becomes tragic because victory may require accepting that the world's sacred architecture is also a machine for repeating sacrifice.

## Local community

- There is no ordinary village here, only attendants, remnant pilgrims, and memory-bearing custodians who treat the approach as a terminal threshold.
- Survivors from earlier temples arrive in half-pilgrimage, half-appeal, hoping the party will choose continuity over rupture.
- The final region is populated by echo-figures of prior centuries: attendants, guardians, and witnesses rather than a normal civic economy.

## Keeper / order

- The Tree keepers are not a conventional priesthood.
- They are the last administrators of a burdened living system and include:
  - preservers who believe continuity is the highest good
  - truth-keepers who want the full history spoken before any final choice
  - mercy advocates who think the system should end even if the world becomes less stable for a time
- Their conflict is the strongest representation of the game's endgame moral dilemma.

## Surface quest

- Ascend the final approach by aligning broken memorial gates, root-bridges, and old oath chambers.
- Reconstruct the sequence of the original sacred burden using fragments recovered from earlier temples.
- Decide whether to preserve the Tree Palace's current function long enough to reach the ending, or force its old systems open before the finale.

## Hidden history

- The temple network was designed as distributed stewardship over an unbearable cost.
- Older guardians were selected, trained, and then erased from the public legend so the world could continue trusting the symbol instead of the labor.
- The Mana Tree persists because generations of protectors accepted becoming part of the mechanism that kept it alive.

## Party focus

- Randi faces the core ideological question: is saving the world worth preserving a system built on hidden sacrifice?
- Primm confronts the scale of stewardship versus freedom and sees how duty can swallow consent.
- Popoi understands that memory, lineage, and burden are not separate things in this world.

## Region state change

- Earlier temple outcomes determine which truths are visible in the final ascent.
- The final region reacts differently depending on whether the party exposed the temple network's history or only stabilized it.
- The ending gains additional emotional weight because the player can now see the cost structure behind the world's sacred order.

## Art signatures

- Root-cathedral architecture, living wood fused with old stone, and high-canopy light shafts.
- Memorial tablets, oath rings, guardian alcoves, and suspended root bridges.
- The tree itself should read as both holy organism and infrastructure.
- VFX language: pulse-light through bark, drifting seed-glow, and layered ancestral silhouettes.
